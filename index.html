What is Digital Literacy?

Digital literacy is crucial for effective participation in today's information-driven society, while cyber safety focuses on protecting individuals and data from online threats. These two concepts are increasingly intertwined, with efforts being made globally to enhance both through various initiatives and policies.

Why do we help educate?

Older adults with lower digital literacy are more prone to falling for fraudulent emails and robocalls. Many older adults may not be familiar with the latest online threats, such as pig butchering scams or AI voice scams. The rapid pace of technological change can be overwhelming, making it harder for older individuals to identify sophisticated scams that use fake login pages, spoofed caller IDs, or deepfake audio. ​Limited digital protection skills significantly increase the likelihood of victimization after exposure to online fraud.

How do they attack?

Impersonation Scams

Impersonation scams deceive older adults by posing as trusted people or organizations. They include fake government or business alerts, emotional “grandparent” emergencies, and long-term romance schemes—all designed to create urgency, gain trust, and steal money or personal information.

Tech Support Scams

Tech support scams occur when criminals pose as tech support to access devices, install malware, or charge for fake services. They often start with alarming pop-ups or calls about viruses. A newer version includes fake “refunds” where scammers claim they overpaid and ask for money back.

Online Shopping & Fake Check Scams

Online shopping scams involve fake purchases or fees that pressure victims into irreversible payments, while fake check scams use convincing counterfeit checks to prompt victims to send money back before the fraud is discovered. Together, these schemes exploit urgency and trust to steal money before victims realize they’ve been deceived.

Status in the Philippines

Challenges in Digital Literacy

The Philippines has made progress in digital transformation, but challenges remain, with about 65% of Filipinos still lacking internet access in 2023. While teachers and future educators report a good level of digital literacy, fully integrating these skills into the education system is still a work in progress.

National Cybersecurity Plan

The DICT leads the country’s cybersecurity efforts through the National Cybersecurity Plan (NCSP) 2023–2028, which aims to create a secure and trustworthy cyberspace aligned with the Philippine Development Plan. The NCSP uses a risk-based approach to address cyber threats, supported by policy assessments and annual cyber exercises. It focuses on strengthening national digital defenses, promoting cybersecurity awareness, providing secure tools for government workers, empowering youth, and addressing vulnerabilities in technology, processes, and human behavior.

Government Measures and Challenges

The DICT has strengthened e-government in the Philippines by unifying cybersecurity and anti-cybercrime efforts with agencies like the PNP. While the SIM Card Registration Act enhances cyber safety, it has also reduced mobile connections and shifted usage habits, creating challenges for businesses. Ongoing cyber threats show the need for stronger partnerships and improved cybersecurity across all sectors.
